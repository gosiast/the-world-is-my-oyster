I almost didn't go to Lisbon. The flights were too cheap to ignore, but the timing felt wrong — the kind of wrong you manufacture when you're scared of actually doing the thing. I booked it anyway, with three days notice and a one-page itinerary I'd typed up on the bus.

## Arriving

The taxi from the airport winds through hills you don't expect. Lisbon is a city of elevations — *miradouros* perched above the rooftops, trams that crawl upward at impossible angles, neighbourhoods that fold into each other like a crumpled map. I checked into a small guesthouse in Mouraria and immediately got lost trying to find dinner.

Getting lost was the best decision I made.

## Food, Always Food

Portuguese food rewards patience. The *pastel de nata* at [Pastéis de Belém](https://pasteisdebelem.pt) — warm, dusted with cinnamon — is worth the queue. But the better meals were the ones I stumbled into: a family-run *tasca* off a side street, menus handwritten on a chalkboard, a fisherman at the next table who shared his wine.

> The best travel meals are the ones you'd never find in a guidebook.

## What I'd Tell You

- Give yourself one full day with no plans. Just walk.
- Ride Tram 28 once, but don't bother fighting the crowds to do it twice.
- The Alfama district is best at dusk, when the light turns everything honey-gold.
- Order the grilled sardines. Order them again.

## The Last Morning

On my final morning I woke before the city and walked down to the waterfront. The Tagus was flat and silver, and the suspension bridge looked almost like the Golden Gate in the early haze. A fisherman was reeling in something slow. Two cats argued over a paper bag.

I didn't want to leave — which is probably the only measure of a good trip that matters.
