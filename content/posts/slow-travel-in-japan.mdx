Most people do Japan at pace. The itineraries I read online involved bullet trains every two days, ten cities in two weeks, a spreadsheet of temples ranked by Instagram potential. I understood the impulse — Japan is an embarrassment of riches — but something about that approach felt like eating a meal too fast.

I gave myself three weeks and decided to barely move.

## Kyoto for Two Weeks

I rented an apartment in the Fushimi ward — not where most people stay, but quiet, residential, and ten minutes from a train line that connected everything I needed. My days organised themselves around a coffee shop on a street I'd never have found otherwise, a supermarket where I learned which prepared foods to trust, and long circular walks that took in whatever the route suggested.

The point wasn't to see Kyoto. The point was to *be* in it for long enough that it stopped being a subject and became a place.

## What Slowing Down Reveals

When you're moving fast, you see surfaces. When you slow down, you start to notice:

- The same retired man walking the same route at 7am, stopping to water the plants in front of shops that aren't his
- How the light in the Fushimi Inari torii gates changes completely depending on the time of day and whether anyone else is there
- That the best *ramen* I had was from a place with four seats and a handwritten card in the window — found on my fifth walk down the same street
- The way Japanese convenience stores are actually extraordinary, and that you could eat well from one every single day

## A Note on Temples

I visited fewer than you'd expect. The ones I did visit, I stayed in for a long time — sitting, watching people, reading the informational plaques slowly instead of photographing them. Ryōan-ji's rock garden, famous enough to be crowded, becomes something different if you find a bench at the edge and wait for the groups to move on.

> Patience is a travel strategy.

## Osaka for One Week

The last week I moved to Osaka, which felt like switching from a minor key to a major one. Louder, more chaotic, more neon. Dotonbori at night is exactly as absurd as the pictures suggest. Takoyaki from a street stall at 11pm is one of the better meals I've had anywhere.

I ate a lot. I walked everywhere. I didn't plan a single day.

## What I'd Do Differently

Nothing, really. Maybe start slower.
